made halt at Bedriacum (a), a village si­

tuated between Verona and Cremona, and

unhappily famous for the slaughter of two

Roman armies. About the same time the

the enemy. Marti us Macer fought with

make head against him were put to the

sword; the rest fled with precipitation to

Cremona. Macer was not willing to lose

by rashness the fruit of his victory. He

knew that, by the arrival of fresh forces,

the fortune of the day might be changed,

and, for that reason, recalled his men from
