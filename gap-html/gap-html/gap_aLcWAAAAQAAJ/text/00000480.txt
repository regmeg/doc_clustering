The successive steps of the elevation of Constantine from his first

assuming the purple at York, to the resignation of Licinius at Nico-

media, have been related with some minuteness and precision, not only

as the events are in themselves both interesting and important, but

still more as they contributed to the decline of the empire by the

expence of blood and treasure, and by the perpetual increase as well

of the taxes as of the military establishment. The foundation of

Constantinople, and the establishment of the Christian religion, were

the immediate and memorable consequences of this revolution.
